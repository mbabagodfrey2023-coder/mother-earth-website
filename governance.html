// THE THREE-LAYER ARCHITECTURE

Three things, stacked.

Together they form what no traditional company structure can: an institution whose mission is constitutionally locked.

Purpose Trust

A Kenyan trust established under the Trustees (Perpetual Succession) Act Cap. 164. Its stated purpose is environmental protection, planetary intelligence, and the sustainable systems Mother Earth supplies.

Trusts of this kind exist for a purpose — not for shareholders. There is no equity to distribute. Profits serve the mission, not investors.

"The Trust exists for a purpose. The mission is the only shareholder."
Golden Share

A single Class B non-transferable share in Mother Earth Kenya Environmental AI Ltd, held in trust by the Purpose Trust. It carries one constitutional right: veto over any decision compromising environmental objectives.

It cannot be sold, transferred, diluted, or overridden by majority vote. Removal requires Trust dissolution — which itself requires the Trustees' unanimous consent.

"No acquisition can hollow out the mission."
Protected Matters

Nine specific decision categories codified in the Trust Deed where the Golden Share automatically activates. Listed in the next section.

Anything from changing the mission statement, to selling environmental data on terms inconsistent with the mission, to amending Trust governance — all locked behind the veto.

"The list is short. The list is final. The list is the firewall."

// FREQUENTLY ASKED

What investors and regulators actually ask.

Click any question to expand. If your question isn't here, ask ECHO on the bottom-right of this page.

If profits don't go to shareholders, how do investors get returns?

They don't. Mother Earth Kenya does not raise equity capital from external investors. The operating company is funded by IRIS marketplace revenue (subscription tiers) and grant capital. Investors who want exposure to the planetary intelligence space can purchase IRIS data subscriptions, partner on specific projects, or fund individual chapter launches — but they do not buy equity that pays dividends or rises in valuation.

Can a future board simply revoke the Golden Share?

No. The Golden Share's existence and rights are codified in the company's Articles of Association — which cannot be amended without Trustee consent (Protected Matter 9.5). Even a 100% replacement of the company's directors cannot remove it. The only way to revoke the Golden Share is to dissolve the Trust, which itself requires Trustee unanimity and is also a Protected Matter (9.7).

Who can actually be acquired here — and what happens if someone tries?

An acquirer can attempt to buy Class A ordinary shares of the operating company. If they reach 50%+ effective control, Protected Matter 9.3 activates immediately and the Golden Share vetoes the transaction. The acquirer ends up holding shares with no decision rights over environmental matters. In practice, the structure makes Mother Earth Kenya economically un-acquirable for any acquirer whose interest is incompatible with the environmental mission.

How does this compare to a B-Corp or a Foundation-owned company?

A B-Corp is a certification — it's a commitment that can be voluntarily abandoned. A Foundation-owned company (e.g. Patagonia's structure) is closer — but Foundations typically have broader fiduciary duties than a single-purpose Trust, which means mission drift is possible. The Kenyan Purpose Trust is narrower and stronger: the Trust's stated purpose is environmental, and Trustees breach their fiduciary duty if they consent to any decision against it.

What stops the founder from going rogue and undoing this himself?

Three things. First, the Trust Deed makes the founder a Trustee bound by fiduciary duties — undoing the mission would be a breach he could be personally sued for. Second, the Protector role activates if the founder becomes absent, incapacitated, or non-compliant. Third, within 12 months of Trust registration, two independent co-Trustees are added — meaning the founder cannot make unilateral decisions on Protected Matters even while in good standing.

Why Kenya?

Three reasons. The Trustees (Perpetual Succession) Act Cap. 164 is one of the cleanest Purpose Trust statutes available globally. The Kenya SEZ Act offers favourable corporate residency for a planetary-mission entity. And the VASP Act 2025 provides a clear regulatory pathway for the blockchain-anchored data marketplace (IRIS). Together they form a uniquely suitable jurisdiction for this combination of trust law, corporate vehicle, and virtual-asset operation.

How does the global chapter model interact with this?

Every chapter must replicate the structure in its own jurisdiction: a local Purpose Trust holding a Golden Share over a local operating entity. No chapter is recognised as a Mother Earth chapter without this. Federated governance (via SAGE agents across chapters) handles cross-border decisions. No chapter can override another's sovereignty. Disputes go to a global Trustee panel comprising one representative per chapter.
